About Cyclone Center

The Dvorak Technique

Prior to the 1960s, tropical cyclones were rarely observed unless they moved close to populated areas, happened to move near an unfortunate ship or occurred in the North Atlantic or Western Pacific (where routine aircraft flights monitored tropical weather systems). Fortunately, geostationary satellites were developed and launched during the 1960s. These satellites orbit the earth at very high altitudes (~22,300 miles or 35,900 km), allowing them to orbit at the same speed as the earth rotates. This allows for the satellite to always “see” the same large area and thus continuously monitor the oceans for tropical cyclone activity.

Despite this important advance, scientists and forecasters still had little hope of determining how strong the winds were (“intensity”) in a tropical cyclone from just looking at a picture of it. Only a very small fraction of tropical cyclones were measured directly by specially designed aircraft. However, it soon became apparent to a gentleman named Vernon Dvorak that the intensity of a tropical cyclone could be skillfully approximated by simply analyzing the cloud patterns and temperatures from a single satellite image. Dvorak developed and improved his method (now called the “Dvorak Technique”) in 1978.

The technique consists of a set of 10 steps, which can be simplified to produce the answers to four important questions:

  1. Where is the center of the system?
  2. What type of cloud pattern best describes the system?
  3. How organized or intense is the cloud pattern?
  4. Does the system look stronger or weaker than 24 hours ago?

Sometimes the answers to these questions are not clear, making the application of the Dvorak technique subjective. Tropical cyclone analysts and forecasters must be trained to successfully apply the many rules and constraints that the technique makes. Even then, experts frequently disagree, which has led to numerous inconsistencies in the tropical cyclone record.

The Dvorak technique was quickly adopted by many of the world’s tropical cyclone forecast centers and remains today as one of the most important tools for estimating tropical cyclone intensity. In this project, you are using a version of the Dvorak technique for classifying tropical cyclones that have formed since the early 1980s. We hope that the application of thousands of classifiers will help resolve the differences in the global tropical cyclone record and improve our understanding of how the nature of these storms may have changed through time.

The team

Cooperative Institute for Climate and Satellites

The Cooperative Institute for Climate and Satellites North Carolina (CICS-NC), inaugurated 2009 and established by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, is an academic non-profit research institute that focuses on enhancing the collective interdisciplinary understanding of the state and evolution of the full Earth System.

NOAA'S National Climatic Data Center (NCDC)

NCDC maintains the world's largest climate data archive and provides climatological services and data to every sector of the United States economy and to users worldwide.  The archive contains paleoclimatic data to centuries-old journals to data less than an hour old.  The Center's mission is to preserve these data and make them available to the general public, business, industry, government, and researchers.

University of North Carolina Asheville

UNC Asheville, the only designated public liberal arts university in North Carolina, offers more than 30 majors ranging from chemistry, mathematics and environmental studies to art, new media and international studies. The university is known for its award-winning undergraduate research and humanities programs.

The Zooniverse

The Zooniverse and the suite of projects it contains is produced, maintained and developed by the Citizen Science Alliance. The member institutions of the CSA work with many academic and other partners around the world to produce projects that use the efforts and ability of volunteers to help scientists and researchers deal with the flood of data that confronts them.

Eye

What does this storm look like?

Look for an area of warmer color near the center. The eye is sometimes pink or gray. It is always a warmer color than the surrounding eye wall.

How can I identify a strong Eye storm?

There will be a warmer and more circular eye at the center, with colder clouds surrounding the eye. The spiral bands will be more pronounced.

Embedded Center

What does this storm look like?

Look for an area of rounded cold clouds near the center of the storm. Strong embedded center storms may start to develop an eye, while weaker storms are more closely related to curved bands.

How can I identify a strong Embedded Center?

There will be spiral bands that are more tightly wrapped and colder (bluer) clouds.

Curved Band

What does this storm look like?

Look for a storm shaped like a comma. They are closely related to weaker embedded center storms, but the tail of the comma is just as cold and large as the head.

How can I identify a stronger curved band?

There will be colder clouds and tightly wrapped spiral bands.

Shear

What does this storm look like?

Look for a swirl in the gray clouds, as well as cold clouds very close to warm ones.

Sheared storms tend to have one side of the cold clouds appear flattened.

How can I identify a strong shear?

The center of the gray swirls will be closer to cooler (blue) clouds, and the colder clouds will be more spiraled and organized.

Other Storms

Post-Tropical

As storms move toward the poles they become less tropical and more like midlatitude weather systems. These storms will have more gray and pink clouds and may appear stretched out with long tails of clouds.

Edge

These storms are on the edge of the satellite's peripheral vision and will often look distorted or have missing data (black areas).

Only select this if the image distortion makes a classification impossible.

No Storm

These storms appear disorganized and are too weak to classify.

What is the eye edge?

The eye of a tropical cyclone is a warm, circular area at the center of the storm surrounded by towering, cold clouds (the “eyewall”). The eye has a narrow diameter at the bottom and slopes outward with height like a giant stadium.

The eye edge is the top of the “stadium”. As the clouds slope outward, they become dramatically colder very quickly.

The edge of the eye occurs where the temperatures (colors) stop changing quickly.

Coldest color surrounding the eye

The eye of a tropical cyclone consists of a swirl of low warm clouds surrounded by towering cold clouds (the “eyewall”).

The clouds of the eyewall must completely surround the eye, but do not have to be any minimum thickness.

How is this determined?

Look for a solid ring of any thickness completely surrounding the eye.

Coldest color least 0.5° thick

Tropical cyclones are more intense when the coldest clouds extend farther out from the center of the system. By identifying the coldest color meeting a minimum thickness, we are able to determine the storm's approximate intensity. The color meeting the minimum thickness may be interrupted with pockets of other colors so long as they are completely engulfed within the dominate band.

How is this determined?

Click and drag your mouse over the image to reveal a 0.5° measuring tool. When the thinnest part of a band fills the tool, you'll know it's at least 0.5° thick.

Banding Features

What is this?

You're looking for a band of cold clouds spiraling into the circular clouds near the center.

Storms with long spirals wrapping around them tend to be stronger.

How do I identify this?

The band must be orange or colder.

It should be separated from the circular center by a wedge that’s red or warmer.

Focus on the length of the band (how far it spirals around) when picking the matching image.
